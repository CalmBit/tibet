Tibetan is an Ergative–absolutive language, as opposed to English (and most other Western languages), which are Nominative–accusative. The actual difference is somewhat difficult to explain, but in short it relates to the way that transitive and intransitive verbs relate to nouns as subjects and objects.

In a Nominative–accusative language, like our own, the Agent of a verb clause (i.e. "I" in "I threw the ball") is marked the same as the the subject in any other clause (i.e. "I have arrived") but is distinct from the object (i.e. "He saw me" vs "He saw I").

An Ergative–absolutive language reverses this dynamic. The subject of an intransitive verb is marked differently to the agent of a transitive verb, and instead shares its marking with the object of the transitive verb. This is hard to formalize into English, as our grammar doesn't necessarily support this.

The Existential copula is used to denote that circumstances or physical things exist or are owned by the speaker, whilst the Equational copula is used to denote value or quality. Personal denotes the statement is in reference to a quality of the speaker. Factual, as the word implies, suggests a statement that is based in reality or firm knowledge. Testimonial denotes that the speaker was a witness to the facts at hand, and that it is only based on sensory knowledge.
