The Tibetan Language - Alphabet

Prev: History
Next: Culture

The Tibetan alphabet is, as previously discussed, a script from the Brahmic family of scripts in Asia. Although considered an "alphabet", it does not function like the Latin alphabet: instead, it functions as an abugida, or alphasyllabary. This means that rather than encoding phonemes, like the Latin alphabet, or encoding concrete, distinct syllable sounds, like the Japanese hiragana and katakana, the Tibetan alphabet encodes consonant information as the primary unit, and vowel information as a secondary mark or indication.

Traditionally, each grapheme encodes a single syllable, with an unmodified grapheme marking a consonant plus the -a sound. Vowels are expressed as diacritical marks, either on the top or the bottom of the grapheme. Therefore, the character for the cosonant "k-" (ཀ) already encodes the syllable "ka", but can be modified into "ki" (ཀི), "ku" (ཀུ), "ke" (ཀེ), and "ko" (ཀོ).

Other modifiers exist as punctuation, such as a marker for the beginning of text (༄, known as "yig mgo" or ཡིག་མགོ་), a full stop for the end of a section (།, known as "tshig-grub" or ཚིག་གྲུབ་), another full stop for the end of a topic (༎, known as "don-tshan" or དོན་ཚན་), and an abstract morpheme delimiter1 (་, known as "tseg" or ཙེག་).

1 Morphemes are the smallest concrete units of grammatical meaning. A morpheme delimiter, then, is a character that denotes seperate morphemes. This is comparable to a hyphenation scheme like "un-break-able" in English.
